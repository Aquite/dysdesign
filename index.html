About

This is a website for designers and developers interested in ensuring that the websites and software they create is accessible for people with dyslexia. As you read through the tips, feel free to click the green buttons on the right to see the changes enacted in real time. If you have dyslexia or just want to jump to the end, feel free to click the green "After" button. Otherwise, keep reading!

What is dyslexia?

As designers, it's important to remember that dyslexia is a learning disability, not a visual disability. Portrayals of dyslexia in the media might show letters jumbled around or words read backwards, but people with dyslexia don't 'see' letters backwards. People with dyslexia tend to struggle with decoding characters and words. Neurobiologically, when people with dyslexia read, they utilize different brain pathways and regions than those without dyslexia. Some interventions exists that focus on strengthening these pathways to help people with dyslexia learn to read, but it's important to understand that dyslexia is a broad term and every person with dyslexia has their own unique experience and story to tell. Some people with dyslexia find reading easier than others, and so each person might require their own unique accomodations on the websites they visit.

There are many myths surrounding dyslexia perpetuated through society, like the portrayals in media I discussed before. People with dyslexia are not 'smarter' or 'dumber' than others - dyslexia affects the ability to read. Many people with dyslexia also have dysgraphia or attention deficit hyperactivity disorder, but neither of these things affect intelligence either. The idea of 'inteligence' is also a myth in and of itself. The last myth I'd like to discuss is one surrounding accomodations. Accomodations such as extra time on tests, screen readers, and spellcheck are not crutches or a sign of weakness, but rather a way to ensure everyone has an equal ability to operate within the framework of our society.

Inclusive Design

Here are ways you can support people with dyslexia when building websites:

Fonts

Using sans-serif, monospaced, or roman fonts can increase reading performance. However, it's important to remember that dyslexia is a learning disability, not a visual one. Fonts can sometimes help, but the research is wild and varied. As a guideline, avoid serif fonts, and ensure that letters are distinct and easily readable. Fonts for dyslexia do exist, like dyslexie, but they often serve to perpetuate the myth that dyslexia is visual and frustrate children with dyslexia when this new font doesn't help them read. Applications do exist to let users set their own website fonts, so it's best to let users customize their fonts themselves.

Use bold for emphasis over italics. Studies have shown they are easier for people with dyslexia to read. Italics are very similar to cursive, which people with dyslexia also tend to struggle with, so also avoid cursive-emulating fonts.

Layout

Don't center-align text, instead left-align it. Finding the start of lines can be difficult when they aren't at the same place. Don't justify text either, since equal spacing between letters tends to be easier to read. Column widths should also be around 45 characters. Most adults prefer around 60, but the most common is 100.

If you're not using a monospace font, you can increase the space between words while decreasing the space between letters, but most fonts already strike a good balance. Line spacing should be around 1.5. Most browsers default to 1.1 or 1.2, while Bootstrap defaults to 1.428. If lines are too close together, they become hard to read. If they are too far apart, they seem disconnected.

Visuals

Textured backgrounds can be distracting and make the text difficult to read. Keep it simple. Having extremely high contrast isn't great either. Changing the background to an off-white or a peachy color can go a long way. If you have to use a white background, change the text color to a dark grey.

Navigation

Pages should always include navigation elements, including a table of contents for larger documents. This is useful regardless of the user, but dyslexic people will save time if they only need to read navigation links instead of trawling the page for the content they're looking for

Increasing the size of headers and indenting them can ease in navigation. Reinforcing text with icons can help people with dyslexia filter through text to find the relevant sections

Text to Speech

Including text-to-speech buttons can help dyslexic people by allowing them to listen to the text on your website. Some applications like hewizo allow users to play text on their website. Including skiplinks and alt tags on images can help people with HTML screen readers navigate content.

Personalization

All of the above tips are useful, but the single-best thing you as a website designer can do is allow users to choose for themselves the font, spacing, and color of text on their webpages. Most browsers already have "reading modes", or extensions that allow users to customize any website like this one.

Personas

When testing software, personas are used to test software before running tests with actual users. Personas are fake personalities created by the designers, so they can test out their software from a viewpoint that's not their own. Including one or multiple dyslexic personas can ensure that you are taking the needs of the dyslexic community into account when building websites. When you decide to test your website with actual users, be sure to not only recruit able participants, but also multiple participants with dyslexia to ensure that your website is accessible to them and the different accomodations they might already use.

Resources

If you're a designer in the Pacific Northwest, check out some of these resources!

WCAG
The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines, by the Web Accessibility Initiative, is a set of guidelines that designers and developers can follow to create a standard for accessibility. These provide an overview of many forms of accessibility, not just dyslexia specific.
Children of the Code
A project to raise awareness for dyslexia. Excellent use of built-in programs to help understand words. Try clicking on any word on the page!
Bureau of Internet Accessibility
An organization that provides website audits and information from an accessibility standpoint. They perform free accessibility analyses of websites if given a url. They also include infomation on designing for dyslexia.
Google Fonts
A collection of easy to use, free fonts to add to your webpage. Fonts aren't super important compared to the rest of the advice here, but it can be useful to allow users to choose from a number of different fonts.
Design. Use. Build.
A collection of University of Washington faculty, students, reasearchers, and industry partners interested in Human Computer Interaction and Design.
Access Computing
An organization which helps students with disabilities pursue computing careers. They also list information about the challenges people with dyslexia face when interacting with computers.
What Works Clearinghouse
A group that reviews and aggregates research on dyslexia intervention programs to determine 'what works' when educating.
